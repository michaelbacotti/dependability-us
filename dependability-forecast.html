Risk: VIX 17.48 — Normal volatility favors defined-risk debit spreads over credit spreads as IV crush reduces available premium.

Market Commentary

The S&P 500 surged to a new all-time high of 7,014 on Friday as a convergence of geopolitical de-escalation signals sent risk assets into a broad rally. The index gained over +4.5% in a single session — SPY moving from ~$679 to $710 — as ceasefire optimism swept across markets with unusual velocity.

What Changed Today

President Trump signaled the Iran conflict "should be ending soon," while a separately announced 10-day ceasefire between Israel and Lebanon removed two simultaneous geopolitical risk premia that had been pressuring markets for months. The Nasdaq Composite extended its gain to a 13th straight session — its longest winning streak since 1992. Oil pricesplunged as supply-shock fears reversed, helping consumer discretionary and technology names surge. The dollar strengthened slightly on improved risk appetite as capital rotates back into equities.

VIX and Volatility Regime

At 17.48, the VIX has re-entered normal range — a significant drop from the mid-20s seen just weeks ago. This is a de-escalation regime: geopolitical uncertainty is compressing, and the market is pricing in a continued bullish trend. In normal VIX territory, short premium strategies still work, but credit spreads see diminishing returns as IV crush has already occurred. Debit spreads become more attractive at this stage of the rally.

Preferred Strategy Framework

With VIX at 17.48 (normal range) and the market in a powerful upward momentum session, today favors bull call spreads for leveraged upside with defined risk. The IV crush after this rally means cheap OTM calls can still capture directional moves efficiently. Bull put spreads remain viable for income-oriented positions if you expect SPY to consolidate rather than gap further. Avoid shorting volatility — headlines can still reverse quickly even in a trending rally.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does VIX measure?

The CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) measures the market's expectation of 30-day volatility in the S&P 500. When VIX is elevated (above 20), it signals elevated fear and uncertainty — which typically favors defined-risk option strategies like spreads over naked positions. Learn more

What does DTE mean?

Days to Expiration (DTE) is how many days remain until the options contract expires. Short-dated spreads (7–14 DTE) capture time decay quickly but require the trade to work fast. Learn more
