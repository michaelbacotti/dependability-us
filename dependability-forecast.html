Market Commentary

The S&P 500 closed at a fresh all-time high of 7,023 on Wednesday, clearing the January peak of 7,002 for the first time this year. The breakthrough rally was catalyzed by President Trump's announcement that the US-Iran conflict is "close to over," signaling a potential diplomatic resolution to months of escalating tensions. Broad risk-on sentiment dominated as energy supply-shock fears abated.

What Changed Today

Iranian officials confirmed bilateral nuclear talks are advancing, with Western diplomats expressing cautious optimism about a framework agreement. The geopolitical risk premium that had been sitting on markets for months began unwinding rapidly. Technology and consumer discretionary led the advance, while energy stocks sold off on reduced crude supply-disruption risk. The dollar weakened slightly on improved global risk appetite.

VIX and Volatility Regime

VIX dropped to 18.17 from 18.36 — still elevated, but clearly in a declining phase from the mid-20s seen in March. This is a de-escalation regime, not a crisis. In this environment, short premium strategies (bull put spreads, iron condors) work well. The VIX structural floor appears to be around 15-16 under normal macro conditions.

Preferred Strategy Framework

With SPY at a record $699.94 and the market holding strong momentum, the bull put spread at the $695/$690 level remains the cleanest expression. The elevated VIX (18.17) continues to provide above-average option premium for sellers. Watch resistance at SPY $705 (SPX 7,050) — a break above that could trigger further short-covering and momentum buying.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does VIX measure?

The CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) measures the market's expectation of 30-day volatility in the S&P 500. When VIX is elevated (above 20), it signals elevated fear and uncertainty — which typically favors defined-risk option strategies like spreads over naked positions. Learn more

What does DTE mean?

Days to Expiration (DTE) is how many days remain until the options contract expires. Short-dated spreads (7–14 DTE) capture time decay quickly but require the trade to work fast. Learn more
