<h3>Risk Management Fundamentals</h3>
<p>Every trade needs a plan: max loss, target profit, time horizon, and a clear thesis. Disciplined traders define acceptable risk before entering — not after. Know your max loss per trade, per day, and per week, and treat those limits as non-negotiable.</p>

<h3>Position Sizing Rules</h3>
<p>Never risk more than 1–2% of your portfolio on a single defined-risk trade. This caps your downside while allowing winners to compound. Position sizing is the only risk variable you fully control; strategy selection is secondary.</p>

<h3>The Greeks and Risk</h3>
<p>Delta tells you directional exposure; gamma tells you how fast that exposure changes; theta is your daily income from selling premium; vega measures your sensitivity to IV shifts. Each Greek quantifies a dimension of risk — ignore them at your peril.</p>

<h3>Drawdown Management — Limiting Portfolio Damage</h3>
<p>A 20% drawdown requires a 25% gain to recover. A 50% drawdown requires a 100% gain. Capping drawdowns early is the highest-leverage action in portfolio management. Stop losses, trailing exits, and systematic de-risking keep you alive through bear markets.</p>
<a href="/risk-management/" class="btn btn-secondary">Learn more →</a>

<h3>Trading Psychology — Managing Fear and Greed</h3>
<p>The biggest risk in trading isn't the market — it's you. Pre-commit to exits before entering a trade. After a loss, resist the urge to over-size the next trade to "make it back." After a win, resist the urge to expand position sizes. Emotional discipline is a skill; it must be trained like any other.</p>

<h3>Iron Condor Risk/Reward</h3>
<p>A typical iron condor might risk $1 per share to earn $0.30–$0.50, with a probability of profit around 60–70%. Over many trades, that edge compounds. But in high-VIX environments, tail risk increases — so wider wings or smaller sizing prevents a single spike from wiping out months of premium.</p>
<a href="/iron-condors-explained/" class="btn btn-secondary">Learn more →</a>

<h3>VIX and Portfolio Hedging — Using Volatility as a Risk Signal</h3>
<p>VIX is a risk meter. When VIX crosses above 20, consider reducing position sizes or adding portfolio hedges (puts, put spreads). When VIX crosses above 30, the market is signaling elevated uncertainty — survival mode. Raise cash, tighten stops, and avoid short premium strategies in that environment.</p>

<h3>⚠️ Risk Rules in Practice — What Happens When Rules Get Broken</h3>
<p><strong>The math:</strong> Start with $10,000. Risk 10% on one trade = $1,000. Lose 10 consecutive trades = your account is down to $3,486. To get back to $10,000 from $3,486 requires a <strong>187% return</strong> — in the same market, with the same strategies.</p>
<p><strong>The emotional trap:</strong> After a loss, the instinct is to bet bigger to recover fast. This is the opposite of what works. Small, consistent losses keep you in the game. One blowup takes you out of the game entirely.</p>
<p><strong>The size rule:</strong> If a trade keeps you up at night, it's too big. Cut it in half until it doesn't.</p>

<h3>⚡ The Two Rules of Risk Management</h3>
<p>
Rule #1: never risk more than you can afford to lose on a single trade. Rule #2: always know your max loss before entering. These aren't suggestions — they're the foundation everything else is built on.
