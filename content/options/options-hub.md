<h3>Options Basics</h3>
<p>Before trading options, understand the building blocks. A call gives you the right to buy at the strike price; a put gives you the right to sell. Expiration dates, strike prices, and intrinsic vs. extrinsic value are the vocabulary of options trading.</p>

<h3>Covered Calls</h3>
<p>Generate income from stocks you already own by selling call options against your position. When the stock stays below the strike, you keep the premium. When it rises above the strike, you're obligated to sell — but at a price you pre-selected.</p>

<h3>Cash Secured Puts</h3>
<p>Want to buy a stock at a discount? Sell a put and collect premium while you wait. If the stock drops below your strike, you're assigned — but at a price lower than you would have paid. If it stays above, you keep the premium and can repeat the process.</p>

<h3>Iron Condors</h3>
<p>Iron condors are a defined-risk, neutral-range-bound income strategy. Sell both a call spread and a put spread on the same underlying, collecting premium when the stock stays within your chosen range. Best deployed when IV is elevated and you expect the stock to drift sideways.</p>

<h3>Iron Condor vs Iron Butterfly</h3>
<p>Both are neutral income strategies, but they differ in structure and risk profile. The condor has wider wings and earns less premium per spread; the butterfly has a tighter center and higher profit potential if the stock lands near the short strike at expiration.</p>

<h3>Bull Put Spread</h3>
<p>A defined-risk bullish strategy that collects premium while capping your upside. Sell a higher strike put and buy a lower strike put as protection. Ideal when you expect a stock to grind upward but want to reduce the cost of downside protection.</p>

<h3>The Greeks — Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega</h3>
<p><strong>Delta (Δ)</strong> measures how much an option's price moves for every $1 move in the stock. A delta of 0.30 means if the stock rises $1, your option gains roughly $0.30 — making it useful for choosing strikes. OTM options have lower delta (0.10–0.30), ATM options cluster around 0.50, and ITM options approach 1.0.</p>
<p><strong>Gamma (Γ)</strong> measures how fast delta changes as the stock moves. Gamma is highest for at-the-money options near expiration, meaning delta can shift violently with small stock moves. Short-dated options carry the most gamma risk — a 7-day option can see its delta swing from 0.20 to 0.80 on a single trading day.</p>
<p><strong>Theta (Θ)</strong> is time decay — the daily cost of holding an option. When you buy options, theta works against you (you lose money every day just from time passing). When you sell options, theta works for you (you collect that daily decay as profit). Selling theta is the foundation of income strategies like covered calls and iron condors.</p>
<p><strong>Vega (V)</strong> measures sensitivity to implied volatility (IV). A 1-point increase in IV — say from 20% to 21% — adds roughly that option's vega to its price. Vega is largest for at-the-money options with more time to expiration, making long-dated ATM options especially sensitive to volatility swings.</p>
<p>Mastering the Greeks separates disciplined traders from gamblers.</p>

<h3>Weekly Options — SPY Weekly Strategy</h3>
<p><strong>SPY weekly options</strong> expire every week, offering fast premium cycles for income-focused traders. The trade-off is less time for the thesis to develop, requiring tighter risk management and smaller position sizes. Best used in low-VIX environments with clear technical levels.</p>
<p><strong>Gamma spike risk:</strong> In the final 24–48 hours before expiration, gamma explodes for near-ATM options. A 1-day option can move 50% or more on a single small stock move — this is extremely dangerous for option buyers. If you're buying weekly options, close positions by Wednesday to avoid the gamma squeeze.</p>

<h3>Position Sizing</h3>
<p>How much capital do you allocate per trade? A rule-based approach — capping risk at 1–2% of total portfolio per position — ensures a single loss doesn't derail your account. Position sizing is the single most important risk management tool available to retail traders.</p>
