<h1>Cash-Secured <em>Puts</em></h1>
<p >Using put options to buy quality stocks at a discount — with a safety net.</p>
</header>

<section class="section">
<div class="content-block">
<h2>How Cash-Secured Puts Work</h2>
<p>A cash-secured put is a sold put option where you set aside enough cash to buy the underlying stock at the strike price if assigned. You collect a premium in exchange for agreeing to buy the stock at the strike price — regardless of where the market price is — if the put buyer chooses to exercise.</p>
<!-- AdSense Vertical -->
<!-- AdSense -->
<p>Unlike naked or uncovered puts, cash-secured means you have the capital to fulfill the obligation. This is not a margin strategy — it is a disciplined approach to either acquire stock at a price you find attractive, or earn premium income while waiting for that price to become available.</p>
</div>
</section>

<section class="section">
<h2 >Strike Selection</h2>
<p >The strike price is your entry price — choose it like you mean it.</p>
<div class="resource-cards">
<div class="resource-card">
<div class="resource-category">Strike Choice</div>
<h3>Out-of-the-Money (OTM) Puts</h3>
<p>Selling a put below the current stock price — for example, selling a $45 put when the stock trades at $50 — means you are only obligated to buy if the stock drops significantly. You collect less premium, but you have a wider buffer before assignment. This approach is ideal when you want to own the stock at a meaningful discount but want to reduce assignment probability.</p>
</div>
<div class="resource-card">
<div class="resource-category">Strike Choice</div>
<h3>At-the-Money (ATM) Puts</h3>
<p>Selling a put at or near the current price collects maximum premium. It also means you are willing to buy the stock at essentially today's price. This makes sense when you genuinely want to build a position and are indifferent between buying today or accumulating over time via the put strategy. The premium effectively reduces your cost basis.</p>
</div>
<div class="resource-card">
<div class="resource-category">Strike Choice</div>
<h3>In-the-Money (ITM) Puts</h3>
<p>Selling a put above the current stock price creates a higher obligation relative to today's price. You collect more premium upfront, but you may be assigned immediately or soon after. This approach is more aggressive and used when you strongly believe the stock will not fall further — or when you want to generate maximum premium income with a strong conviction on direction.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div>
<p><strong>The practical rule:</strong> Sell cash-secured puts at a strike price where you would genuinely be comfortable owning the stock. If the put is assigned, you should feel like you bought at a fair or favorable price — not that you were forced into a position.</p>
</div>
</section>

<section class="section">
<h2 >Assignment Risk</h2>
<p >Assignment is not a failure — it's a feature of the strategy.</p>
<div class="content-block">
<p>When a put is assigned, you are required to buy the underlying stock at the strike price. This is only a problem if you did not want to own the stock at that price. When done correctly, assignment means you acquired the stock at exactly the price you targeted, with a lower effective cost basis than if you had bought it outright.</p>
<p>Assignment risk increases as the stock falls toward and below the strike price, particularly near expiration. This is why many traders prefer to sell puts with 30–45 days to expiration, giving the position time to work and reducing the "pin risk" of short-dated options expiring right at the money.</p>
<p>You can reduce assignment risk by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Selling OTM puts rather than ATM or ITM puts</li>
<li>Using a shorter time horizon (weekly or monthly expirations) so you can reassess frequently</li>
<li>Monitoring positions and taking action before expiration if the stock has moved significantly</li>
</ul>
</div>
</section>

<section class="section">
<h2 >Rolling Positions</h2>
<p >When to extend, adjust, or close a position.</p>
<div class="content-block">
<p>Rolling a put option means closing the current position and opening a new one with a later expiration — and typically a different strike. This is done to manage positions where the stock has moved against you and you want more time for it to recover, or to extend the duration of premium collection.</p>
<p>There are two basic roll scenarios:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Roll down and out:</strong> The stock has risen, and your put is far OTM. You can roll to a lower strike and later expiration to collect more premium while waiting for a pullback.</li>
<li><strong>Roll out:</strong> The stock has fallen toward your strike. You extend the expiration to give it more time, often at a credit (closing the near-term put at a gain and selling a further-out put for more premium).</li>
</ul>
<p>Rolling should be intentional — not a reflexive response to being wrong. If the fundamental thesis has changed and the stock is no longer attractive at the strike price, it may be better to take the assignment or close at a loss than to roll indefinitely into a deteriorating position.</p>
</div>
</section>

<section class="section">
<h2 >Building Positions at Lower Costs</h2>
<p >The systematic approach to accumulating stock over time.</p>
<div class="content-block">
<p>One of the most powerful uses of cash-secured puts is as a stock accumulation tool. Instead of buying shares at today's price, you sell puts at your target buy price. If the stock falls to your strike, you get assigned and own the shares at that price — plus you collected premium. If the stock doesn't fall there, you keep the premium and try again next month.</p>
<p>This approach works best with high-quality companies you want to own long-term. You set a price target based on fundamental analysis or technical support — not emotion. Over time, this "laddering" strategy results in an average cost that is consistently below the average price at which the stock traded.</p>
<p>For example: A stock trading at $100. You sell a $90 put for $2.00 premium. If assigned, your effective cost is $88 per share. If not assigned, you keep the $200 premium and look for the next opportunity. Repeat monthly. Over 12 months of rolling puts, premium collected can materially reduce your effective cost basis even in flat or slightly upward trending markets.</p>
</div>
<div>
<p><strong>The accumulation mindse</strong>t treats each put sale as an independent decision. Whether you get assigned or not, you earned premium for your willingness to buy. The goal is not to be right about direction — it is to collect consistent income while building a position at prices you find attractive.</p>
</div>
