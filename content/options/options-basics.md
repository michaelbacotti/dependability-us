<h1>Options Trading <em>Basics</em></h1>
<p >A clear-eyed introduction to calls, puts, and the essential vocabulary of options trading.</p>
</header>

<section class="section">
<div class="content-block">
<h2>What Is an Option?</h2>
<p>An option is a contract that gives you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell a specific asset at a predetermined price on or before a specific date. Unlike owning shares outright, options are derivative instruments whose value is derived from an underlying asset such as a stock, index, or ETF.</p>
<!-- AdSense Vertical -->
<!-- AdSense -->
<p>The two core building blocks of options are calls and puts. Understanding these two instruments unlocks the rest of the options landscape, from basic income strategies to complex multi-leg structures.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Call Option</h3>
<p>A call gives the holder the right to <strong>buy</strong> the underlying asset at the strike price before expiration. Buying calls is a bullish bet — you profit when the asset rises above the strike price by more than you paid for the premium. Think of it as putting down a deposit on something you expect to appreciate.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Put Option</h3>
<p>A put gives the holder the right to <strong>sell</strong> the underlying asset at the strike price before expiration. Buying puts is a bearish or protective bet — you profit when the asset falls below the strike price. Investors also use puts to insure stock positions against downside moves.</p>
</div>
</section>

<section class="section">
<div class="content-block">
<h2>Key Terminology</h2>
<p>Before placing your first options trade, it pays to understand the language. These terms show up in every option chain, every broker interface, and every strategy discussion.</p>
</div>
<div>
<div>
<h3>Strike Price</h3>
<p>The fixed price at which the option holder can buy (for a call) or sell (for a put) the underlying asset. Also called the exercise price.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Expiration Date</h3>
<p>The date on which the option contract expires and ceases to exist. After expiration, the option is either exercised, sold, or becomes worthless. Weekly, monthly, and quarterly expirations are common.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Premium</h3>
<p>The price you pay to purchase an option contract. It's the market's current price for the option — set by supply, demand, and the "Greeks." Premiums are quoted per share; each contract covers 100 shares.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>In-The-Money (ITM)</h3>
<p>An option with intrinsic value. A call is ITM when the underlying price is above the strike. A put is ITM when the underlying price is below the strike. ITM options typically cost more but have a higher probability of expiring profitably.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Out-of-the-Money (OTM)</h3>
<p>An option with no intrinsic value. A call is OTM when the underlying price is below the strike. A put is OTM when the underlying price is above the strike. OTM options are cheaper but require a larger price move to become profitable.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>At-The-Money (ATM)</h3>
<p>When the underlying price is essentially equal to the strike price. ATM options are maximally sensitive to changes in implied volatility and time decay — making them popular for certain strategies like straddles.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Intrinsic Value</h3>
<p>The immediate, tangible value of an option if exercised right now. For a call: underlying price minus strike price. For a put: strike price minus underlying price. Zero if the option is OTM.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Extrinsic Value</h3>
<p>The portion of an option's premium that exceeds its intrinsic value. Extrinsic value represents time value and implied volatility — the "bet" that the underlying will move before expiration. It erodes every day, a process called time decay.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Bid / Ask</h3>
<p>The bid is the highest price a buyer is willing to pay. The ask is the lowest price a seller will accept. The spread between them is a cost of trading — particularly relevant for less liquid options.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h3>Assignment</h3>
<p>When an option is exercised, the underlying position changes hands at the strike price. If you sold a call and it gets assigned, you may be forced to deliver shares at the strike — even if the current market price is much higher.</p>
</div>
</div>
</section>

<section class="section">
<div class="content-block">
<h2>Why Trade Options?</h2>
<p>Options aren't just leveraged bets on direction. Sophisticated traders use them for income generation, portfolio insurance, and precise risk expression. Here are some of the most common motivations:</p>
<ul class="content-list">
<li><strong>Leverage</strong> — A small premium controls a large amount of underlying stock, amplifying returns (and losses) relative to capital deployed.</li>
<li><strong>Income</strong> — Selling covered calls allows you to collect premium while holding a stock you already own.</li>
<li><strong>Insurance</strong> — Buying puts on a stock or portfolio can protect against catastrophic downside, similar to an insurance premium.</li>
<li><strong>Defined Risk</strong> — Unlike futures or short stock positions, long option buyers cannot lose more than the premium paid.</li>
<li><strong>Direction and Volatility</strong> — Options allow you to trade not just price direction, but the rate at which prices move (implied volatility).</li>
</ul>
</div>
<div class="content-block">
<h2>Important Considerations for Beginners</h2>
<p>Options trading offers powerful capabilities, but it comes with real complexity. Before you begin, keep these principles in mind:</p>
<ul class="content-list">
<li><strong>Time decay works against you</strong> — Every day, the extrinsic value of an option erodes. What seems like a cheap option today can lose most of its value even if the underlying moves in your favor — but not fast enough.</li>
<li><strong>Implied volatility matters as much as direction</strong> — An option can be "right" about the market's direction and still lose money if implied volatility falls. High IV means expensive options; low IV means cheaper ones.</li>
<li><strong>Assignment risk is real</strong> — Selling naked calls or holding short positions in options can result in assignment at inopportune times. Understand the mechanics before selling options.</li>
<li><strong>Paper trade first</strong> — Before committing real capital, track hypothetical trades over several weeks. Watch how premiums move, how earnings announcements affect IV, and how quickly time decay accrues.</li>
</ul>
</div>
