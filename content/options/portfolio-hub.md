<h3>Diversification Basics — Why It Matters</h3>
<p>Diversification doesn't guarantee profits — it limits the damage any single event can inflict on your portfolio. A mix of asset classes (stocks, bonds, alternatives) that don't move in perfect lockstep reduces overall volatility. The goal is a smoother ride: not maximizing peak returns, but surviving drawdowns well enough to stay invested.</p>

<h3>Cash vs. Bonds in a High-Rate Environment</h3>
<p>High interest rates change the calculus. Money market funds and short-term Treasuries now yield 4–5%, making them a viable alternative to long-duration bonds. For capital preservation, short-duration instruments reduce interest rate risk while providing competitive yields — a marked shift from the 2010s when bonds dominated.</p>

<h3>Drawdown Management</h3>
<p>A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover. That's why limiting drawdowns matters more than chasing returns. Pre-defined exit points, trailing stops, and systematic rebalancing help keep your portfolio intact through turbulent markets, preserving capital for the next recovery.</p>

<h3>Using Options for Portfolio Protection</h3>
<p>Protective puts, collar strategies, and put spreads can hedge your equity exposure without liquidating your positions. Buying index puts as portfolio insurance — particularly when VIX is low and options are cheap — is one of the most cost-effective ways to protect against major drawdowns.</p>
<a href="/risk-management/" class="btn btn-secondary">Learn more →</a>

<div class="feature-icon">🔄</div>
<h3>Rebalancing Frequency — When and How</h3>
<p>Quarterly rebalancing is common, but the right frequency depends on your risk tolerance and tax situation. Calendar-based rebalancing (quarterly or annually) is simple; threshold-based rebalancing (triggered when an asset drifts 5%+ from target) is more responsive. Over-rebalancing destroys returns through transaction costs and tax events.</p>
</div>

<h3>Position Sizing Rules</h3>
<p>Never allocate more than 1–2% of total portfolio capital to a single defined-risk trade. For uncorrelated positions, 3–5% may be appropriate. Position sizing is the most direct control you have over portfolio risk — it's more important than which strategy you choose.</p>
<a href="/position-sizing-rules/" class="btn btn-secondary">Learn more →</a>
</div>
<div class="feature-card">
<div class="feature-icon">🧠</div>
<h3>Trading Psychology</h3>
<p>Emotional decision-making is the primary cause of account destruction. Fear after a loss leads to under-sizing or abandoning strategies that still have an edge. Greed after a win leads to over-sizing and blowing up. A written trade plan and pre-set exits remove emotion from the equation.</p>
